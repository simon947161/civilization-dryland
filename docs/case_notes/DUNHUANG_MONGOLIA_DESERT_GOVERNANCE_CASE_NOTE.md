## 1. Background

Recent discussions with Liu Wei, Secretary-General of the Kunlun Mountain Ecological Protection Association, revealed several emerging opportunities related to desertification control, ecological restoration, and international cooperation.

These discussions focused on:

- Dunhuang Yitang Lake Ecological Demonstration Area
- The publication of *The Great Nation's Desertification Control*
- Potential Mongolia Desertification Cooperation Projects
- Future applications of ClimateOS and CCZPS in dryland governance

This note records preliminary observations and does not constitute a project proposal, investment recommendation, or formal commitment.

## 2. Dunhuang Ecological Guardian Initiative

### 2.1 Current Activities

The project is located in the Yitang Lake region near Dunhuang.

Current activities include:

- Desertification control
- Tree planting and vegetation restoration
- Public participation through cloud planting programs
- Cooperation with local forestry and grassland authorities
- Exploration of a long-term ecological demonstration base

The project has already moved beyond a simple planting activity and is gradually evolving toward a broader ecological restoration platform.

### 2.2 Future Potential

The site may eventually support several functions.

#### Ecological Restoration

- Vegetation recovery
- Soil stabilization
- Biodiversity enhancement

#### Public Education

- Environmental education programs
- Citizen participation
- Ecological awareness campaigns

#### Demonstration Functions

- Restoration process documentation
- Ecological outcome verification
- International case presentation

#### Long-Term Monitoring

- Climate observation
- Vegetation monitoring
- Remote sensing assessment
- Ecological archive development

## 3. The Great Nation's Desertification Control

### 3.1 Publication Context

The book *The Great Nation's Desertification Control* was published in 2026.

It summarizes decades of Chinese experience in:

- Desertification control
- Shelterbelt construction
- Ecological engineering
- Community participation
- Long-term environmental governance

### 3.2 Potential Role

The publication may serve as a public communication entry point, a knowledge transfer tool, and a support mechanism for physical demonstration sites such as Dunhuang.

It may help connect ecological stories, technical achievements, and long-term restoration experience into an internationally understandable narrative.

## 4. Mongolia Desertification Cooperation

### 4.1 Initial Information

According to preliminary discussions:

- Mongolia-related project owners are exploring partnerships in China.
- Interest exists in future desertification-control cooperation.
- Current challenges include finding implementation partners willing to accept early-stage project risks.

The project remains at an exploratory stage.

### 4.2 Key Challenges

Mongolia projects involve substantially higher complexity than domestic demonstration projects.

Factors include:

#### Ecological Factors

- Climate variability
- Water availability
- Vegetation suitability

#### Financial Factors

- Long investment cycles
- Maintenance costs
- Return uncertainty

#### Governance Factors

- Policy continuity
- Institutional coordination
- Long-term stewardship

#### International Factors

- Cross-border cooperation
- Cultural differences
- Geopolitical considerations

#### Social Factors

- Community acceptance
- Local participation
- Capacity building

### 4.3 Preliminary Position

The current position is open but cautious.

The opportunity is open because the ecological significance is evident and dryland cooperation may benefit regional sustainability.

The position remains cautious because information is incomplete, long-term governance risks are not yet understood, and local implementation capacity requires further assessment.

Further engagement should be based on additional field information, local stakeholder consultation, and careful assessment of governance, funding, maintenance, and social acceptance risks.

## 8. Concluding Observation

The most important insight from this discussion is that future ecological governance may require not only restoration capability, but also verification capability.

China already possesses significant practical experience in desertification control.

The larger challenge may be the development of:

- long-term monitoring systems,
- evidence-based validation systems,
- internationally understandable communication systems,
- and digital ecological governance tools.

These areas may represent the future contribution space for ClimateOS and CCZPS.
